Hexchess implements **Glinski's hexagonal chess**, the most popular hexagonal chess variant. Here are the key differences from standard chess.

## The board

The board has **91 hexagonal cells** arranged in a hex grid, compared to 64 squares in standard chess. Each cell has 6 edges and up to 6 orthogonal neighbors.

Cells are labeled with **Glinski notation** (see below). The center cell is `f6`.

Unlike a square board with 2 cell colors, the hex board has **3 cell colors**. Each bishop is bound to one color — which is why each side gets 3 bishops instead of 2.

## Notation

Hexchess uses **Glinski algebraic notation** — the standard notation for Glinski's hex chess. Files are letters and ranks are numbers, just like standard chess, but adapted for the hex grid.

- **Files** are 11 columns labeled `a b c d e f g h i k l`. The letter `j` is **skipped** (the same convention used in ICCF correspondence chess) to avoid `i`/`j` confusion. File `f` is the central column.
- **Ranks** are numbered `1`–`11`. Because of the hex shape, each file has a different number of ranks: file `f` has 11 cells (`f1`–`f11`), while edge files `a` and `l` have only 6.

The center cell is **f6**. White's king starts on **g1**, queen on **e1**, with bishops stacked on **f1/f2/f3**.

### Move syntax

Move notation follows standard chess algebraic notation, with no castling:

- **Piece moves** — uppercase piece letter + destination: `Nf4`, `Qe6`, `Kg2`
- **Pawn moves** — destination only: `b3`, `f5`
- **Captures** — use `x`: `bxc3`, `Nxe5`
- **Disambiguation** — when two pieces of the same type can reach a square, prefix with the source file: `Nbd4`
- **Check / checkmate** — append `+` or `#`: `Qe6+`, `Nf4#`
- **Promotion** — append `=` and the promoted piece: `b9=Q` (the 11 promotion cells per side are along the opponent's far edge)
- **En passant** — written like a normal pawn capture (sometimes annotated `e.p.`)
- **No castling** — there is no `O-O` move in Glinski chess


## Pieces and starting position

Each side starts with:
- 1 king, 1 queen, 2 rooks, 2 knights
- **3 bishops** (one per cell color)
- **9 pawns** (not 8)

There is **no castling** in Glinski chess.

## Movement directions

Standard chess has 8 directions (4 orthogonal + 4 diagonal). On a hex board the geometry is different:

### Rook (6 cardinal directions)

The rook slides along the 6 cardinal (orthogonal) directions — through cell edges.

### Bishop (6 diagonal directions)

The bishop slides along the 6 diagonal directions — through cell vertices. Note that diagonals on a hex board skip cells, so each diagonal step moves 2 in one axis and -1 in another.

### Knight (12 jump destinations)

The knight has **12 possible jumps** from the center (vs 8 in standard chess). Like standard chess, it leaps over pieces.

### King (12 adjacent cells)

The king moves one step in any of the 6 cardinal or 6 diagonal directions — **12 possible moves** from the center (vs 8 in standard chess).

## Pawns

- White pawns advance toward higher ranks, black pawns toward lower ranks (white pawns start on `b1, c2, d3, e4, f5, g4, h3, i2, k1`; black pawns all start on rank 7)
- Pawns can capture diagonally forward (2 capture directions, similar to standard chess)
- En passant works analogously to standard chess

## Game-ending conditions

The same conditions as standard chess:
- **Checkmate** — the side to move is in check with no legal moves
- **Stalemate** — the side to move has no legal moves but is not in check (draw)
- **Threefold repetition** — the same position occurs three times (draw)
- **50-move rule** — 50 consecutive moves without a capture or pawn advance (draw)
- **Insufficient material** — neither side can force checkmate (draw)

## Starting position statistics

From the initial position, White has **51 legal moves** — significantly more than standard chess (20 moves). The king has 2 legal moves from the start.
