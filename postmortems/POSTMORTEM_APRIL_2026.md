# April 2026 Postmortem: v1 -> v5 Training Run

## Scope

This postmortem covers the training run from the first promoted bootstrap model (`v1`, exported on 2026-04-17) through the latest rejected candidate (`v5`, evaluated on 2026-04-22). The goal is to explain why we spent substantial compute, trained on more than 10M self-play positions, and still ended with a `v5` candidate that did not beat `v1`.

Artifacts reviewed:

- `models/approved.meta.json`
- `models/latest.meta.json`
- `models/versions/{1..5}.onnx`
- `state/trainer_metrics.json`

- `state/evals/v{1..5}/games/*.json`
- `data/selfplay/v1/*.npz` inventory
- `data/imitation/*.npz` inventory
- Sampled self-play sidecars and traces: `data/selfplay/v1/*.meta.json`, `data/selfplay_traces/v1/*.json`

## TL;DR

The main loop never actually advanced beyond `v1` self-play. We exported `v2`, `v3`, `v4`, and `v5`, but no candidate was approved, so workers kept generating `data/selfplay/v1/...` the entire time. As of 2026-04-22, the S3 inventory showed `108,789` self-play files and `10,149,768` self-play positions, all under `data/selfplay/v1/`. `models/latest.meta.json` pointed to `v5` with `positions_at_promote = 10,003,793`, while `models/approved.meta.json` still pointed to `v1`.

The result was not just “neutral.” `state/evals/v5/gate_summary.json` showed `v5` scoring `22/31/3` against `v1` over `56` games, for a score of `0.420` with 95% CI approximately `[0.293, 0.546]`. Earlier candidates were also inconsistent: `v2 = 0.510`, `v3 = 0.475`, `v4 = 0.520`. We got smooth optimization and clean artifacts, but not a working policy-improvement loop.

## Headline Findings

### 1. We trained on >10M self-play positions, but they were all generated by approved `v1`

Hard evidence:

- `models/approved.meta.json`: `{"version": 1, "timestamp": "2026-04-17T08:19:57.608553+00:00"}`
- `models/latest.meta.json`: `{"version": 5, "timestamp": "2026-04-21T14:19:47.140819+00:00", "positions_at_promote": 10003793}`
- S3 inventory on 2026-04-22:
  - `data/selfplay/v1/`: `108,789` files, `10,149,768` positions
  - `data/imitation/`: `23,291` files, `6,594,752` positions
  - `data/selfplay/v2/` through `data/selfplay/v5/`: no files
- From the first to the last `data/selfplay/v1/*.npz` key (`2026-04-17T08:24:42Z` -> `2026-04-22T04:55:20Z`), overall self-play production averaged about `24.2` positions/sec, `1,452` positions/min, or `87.1k` positions/hour.

Interpretation:

- The trainer exported new candidates roughly every `2.5M` fresh self-play positions, which matches `promote_every_new_positions = 2_500_000` in [training/config.py](/Users/kevz/Desktop/hexchess/training/config.py:58).
- But because approval never moved, self-play never moved. This means the run did not execute true AlphaZero-style policy iteration after `v1`; it executed repeated supervised improvement attempts against a fixed `v1`-generated distribution.

Why this matters:

- More data from a fixed weak policy is not the same thing as stronger self-play. It can stabilize estimates and reduce loss, but it does not automatically create a stronger generator for the next round.

### 2. Candidate strength versus `v1` did not improve monotonically, and `v5` was clearly behind in the observed sample

Direct gate results from `state/evals/v{N}/gate_summary.json`:

| Candidate | Approved Opponent | Games | W-D-L | Score | Status |
| --- | --- | ---: | --- | ---: | --- |
| `v2` | `v1` | 101 | 51-1-49 | 0.510 | rejected |
| `v3` | `v1` | 101 | 45-6-50 | 0.475 | rejected |
| `v4` | `v1` | 100 | 51-2-47 | 0.520 | rejected |
| `v5` | `v1` | 56 | 22-3-31 | 0.420 | rejected |

Notes:

- The gate is not “beat 50%.” It is `GATE_PASS_SCORE = 0.55` in [training/evaluation_service.py](/Users/kevz/Desktop/hexchess/training/evaluation_service.py:49).
- `v2` and `v4` were slightly above 50%, but neither approached the 55% promotion bar.
- `v5` was materially worse than that. As of `state/evals/v5/gate_summary.json` updated `2026-04-22T21:42:48.943745+00:00`, its observed score was below 50%, not merely “insufficient evidence.”

This is the central failure signal. We did not get monotonic policy improvement against the approved model despite five days of compute and >10M self-play positions.

### 7. Observability was materially worse than it should have been

As of the audit on 2026-04-22:

- `logs/trainer/`: no objects
- `heartbeats/`: no objects
- `benchmarks/results/`: no objects

This is partly explained by code:

- S3 JSONL log upload exists via `S3RotatingFileSink`, but it is opt-in in [training/logging_setup.py](/Users/kevz/Desktop/hexchess/training/logging_setup.py:173).
- The trainer calls `setup_json_logging("trainer", run_id=cfg.run_id)` without `s3_sink_path`, so no S3 trainer event log is emitted by default [training/trainer_loop.py](/Users/kevz/Desktop/hexchess/training/trainer_loop.py:784).
- The only durable trainer signal we had was `state/trainer_metrics.json`, which stores summary aggregates.

Impact:

- We can reconstruct high-level loss and promotion cadence.
- We cannot reconstruct step-level dynamics, throughput swings, or exact reasons for any intra-summary instability.
- We also do not have durable worker-heartbeat evidence for cluster health during the run.

## What Went Well

- The pipeline produced clean versioned artifacts. We have immutable ONNX snapshots for `v1` through `v5`, version-scoped eval directories, and enough trainer summaries to reconstruct the overall run.
- The direct gate did its job. `v2` through `v5` were not promoted into self-play, which prevented the loop from switching workers onto a model that had not clearly beaten the approved baseline.
- The sampled self-play data looked structurally sound. No obvious corruption, masking bugs, or target-normalization failures showed up in the sampled files.
- Export cadence was stable. Model snapshots arrived roughly every `25` hours, which is consistent with the configured `2.5M`-position promotion interval and the observed self-play rate.

## What Went Poorly

- We burned compute without getting policy iteration. The run produced `10,149,768` self-play positions, but because approval never advanced, those positions all came from the same approved generator.
- The gate score versus `v1` never improved monotonically. Two candidates were slightly above 50%, one was below, and the latest candidate was clearly behind.
- The benchmark suite did not predict direct match strength well enough. `v5` looked much better versus Heuristic than `v1` but still lost the only comparison that mattered.
- Loss improvement was not enough, and may have been misaligned with practical strength.
- Observability was not good enough for a run of this size. We should not have to infer this much from summary metrics and a handful of JSONs after spending this much compute.

## Best-Effort Root Cause Statement

Evidence-backed statement:

1. The run optimized the network successfully enough to change the exported model, but not enough to produce a stronger head-to-head player against `v1`.
2. The training loop got stuck in a fixed-policy regime after `v1`. The self-play generator never advanced, so most of the compute went into learning from more `v1` data rather than iterating through stronger generations.
3. The artifacts do not point to data corruption as the primary explanation.
4. The artifacts do point to a loop-level failure: objective improvement and anchor improvement did not convert into approved-model improvement.

My current read is that the main failure mode was not “the trainer exploded” or “the data was broken.” It was that we never escaped the `v1` data-generating policy, so the run behaved more like extended offline training on a fixed self-play corpus than an actual self-improving RL loop.

## Speculative Hypotheses And How To Test Them

These are plausible explanations, but they are not proven by the reviewed artifacts.

### Hypothesis A: The fixed `v1` self-play distribution capped improvement

Why it is plausible:

- All self-play stayed under `data/selfplay/v1/`.
- The candidates changed, but the data generator did not.

How to test it:

1. Run a shadow branch where the latest candidate generates self-play without replacing the approved model.
2. Train the next candidate on a mixed corpus: approved self-play plus shadow-candidate self-play.
3. Compare gate results after a fixed fresh-position budget, not after a fixed wall-clock budget.

### Hypothesis B: The benchmark suite is too weak or too misaligned to be useful as a leading indicator

Why it is plausible:

- `v5` scored `0.500` versus Heuristic and `0.188` versus Minimax-2, both above the `v1` baseline, while still scoring `0.420` versus `v1`.

How to test it:

1. Retrospectively run longer direct matches `v2`/`v4`/`v5` versus `v1` and versus each other.
2. Add at least one anchor closer in style and strength to the approved model.
3. Measure rank correlation between benchmark score changes and direct gate score changes across future versions.

### Hypothesis C: The replay window and imitation schedule were too conservative for this phase

Why it is plausible:

- The trainer always counts `N_total` from self-play only and uses a sublinear replay window [training/trainer_loop.py](/Users/kevz/Desktop/hexchess/training/trainer_loop.py:876).
- At `N_total ~= 10.1M`, the replay window is about `1.21M` positions, so the model was training on a sliding subset of `v1` self-play rather than the whole corpus.
- Imitation remained non-zero through `v5` (`~12.9%` by the current schedule in [training/config.py](/Users/kevz/Desktop/hexchess/training/config.py:65)).

How to test it:

1. Run an ablation with imitation shut off earlier, for example after `v2`.
2. Run an ablation with a larger replay window and compare gate score per 1M fresh positions.
3. Keep the compute budget fixed and compare direct gate outcomes, not just loss curves.

### Hypothesis D: The loss stack is not aligned enough with gate strength

Why it is plausible:

- Policy and value losses moved in the right direction while gate strength did not.
- Auxiliary heads improved substantially, but that may not matter for direct match strength.

How to test it:

1. Run a short ablation reducing or removing `aux` and `stv` head weights.
2. Compare equal-budget runs on the next 2.5M fresh positions.
3. Use direct gate score, not training loss, as the decision metric for the ablation.

## Concrete Changes Before The Next Large Run

1. Persist trainer event logs to S3. Wire `s3_sink_path` into trainer startup so the next run has durable JSONL step logs.
2. Persist immutable per-version metadata. Every exported model should get a versioned metadata object with timestamp, `positions_at_promote`, replay-window size, imitation mix, and config snapshot.
3. Keep heartbeats durable during the run. We need to know which workers were alive, what model they were on, and whether throughput flattened.
4. Add a shadow-self-play experiment before spending another 10M positions. The cheapest way to test the main hypothesis is to see whether candidate-generated self-play changes the gate trajectory.
5. Retrospectively extend the `v2` and `v4` gates. Those were the two near-parity candidates; it is worth checking whether they were truly flat or just never close enough to the 55% bar.

## Bottom Line

We did get a real training run: the model changed, the losses moved, the data pipeline stayed structurally healthy, and the evaluation system prevented bad promotions. But the run did not achieve the thing we actually wanted, which was policy iteration.

The strongest evidence-backed explanation is simple: after `v1`, we kept training new candidates on ever more `v1` self-play, and none of those candidates became strong enough to replace `v1`. That is why `>10M` positions turned into “still not better than `v1`” instead of a stronger approved model.
